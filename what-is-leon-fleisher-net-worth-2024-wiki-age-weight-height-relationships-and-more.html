What was a significant impact on his career?

Fleisher developed focal dystonia, a neurological condition, which left him unable to play with his right hand for 30 years, significantly impacting his career.

Did he win any awards?

Yes, among many accolades, Fleisher won the Gold Medal of the Queen Elisabeth Music Competition in 1952 and received the Kennedy Center Honors in 2007.

When did Leon Fleisher pass away?

He passed away on August 2, 2020, at the age of 92.

What was unique about his piano playing later in his career?

After losing the use of his right hand, Fleisher focused on music written for the left hand and eventually regained some use of his right hand, allowing him to perform two-handed repertoire again.

Where did he teach?

Fleisher taught at prestigious institutions such as the Peabody Institute at Johns Hopkins University, the Curtis Institute of Music, and the Royal Conservatory of Music in Toronto.

What genres did he perform in?

He was renowned for his performances in classical music, particularly known for his interpretations of concertos by composers like Mozart, Beethoven, and Brahms.

How did he contribute to music education?

Fleisher had a long association with the Peabody Conservatory of Music, taught at the Curtis Institute of Music, and was a founder of the Theatre Chamber Players at the John F. Kennedy Center.

Did Leon Fleisher have any notable students?

Yes, Fleisher’s students include prominent pianists such as André Watts, Yefim Bronfman, and Hélène Grimaud, showcasing his influence as a teacher.

What was one of his most significant achievements after overcoming his hand condition?

One of Fleisher’s significant achievements was the release of Two Hands, his first solo recording in over 40 years, following the partial recovery of his right hand.
